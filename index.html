April 2, 2026
Airpods 4

Mapping: Mapping is related to layout of controls and how they affect the product. In regard to the air pods they connect seamlessly to all apple devices that are associated with an apple account. On the physical design of device there are touch pods at the end of the earbud that control whether the audio is paused or calls are answers.

MAYA: MAYA is the balance of what makes the product stand out and the familiarity of design. The design of the air pods is simple but a well-known stable considering that they are on their 4th variation. A reason for it standing out is great audio quality, wireless, and seems to handle your everyday phone functions naturally such as calls while also providing entertainment in the form of music/podcasts. It’s the standard earbud design but without the harsh piece of plastic that goes into your ear. It seems to be universal sizing for the air pods coming from someone that often has to change out to the smallest ear bud size.

Aesthetic-Usability Effect: The aesthetic usability effect is the idea that things that are beautiful in design are easier to use. The design of the air pods is simple but pleasing in the sense that they are not overly complicated. There are not a lot of buttons on it or many things to learn about it. For the most part it is connected to Bluetooth and you’re ready to go.

Veblen Effect: A belief that the price equates to the quality. It is a common belief among Americans that you get what you paid for. The prices are displayed below the picture at the top. Although I agree that the air pods are of excellent quality and are simple to integrate into daily life, they are rather pricey.

Sunk Cost Effect: A habit of investing money into something because one has done so in the past. This can be said about the product due to the iterations it has had. Despite not too much changing from generation-to-generation people are still more than willing to buy the latest as soon as they can.

March 7, 2026
buckID card/system
The digital BuckID allows students to manage their campus accounts digitally, including checking meal balances, printing dollars, making deposits, reporting lost or stolen cards, viewing recent transactions, and displaying a digital version of the student ID with a QR code for building entries that allow.
Interaction Description:
A typical interaction involves opening an app (while signed in) to view information and seek out any actions. This can all be managed through a web browser as well. Additionally, ordering food at dining halls can be done when linking the BuckID to a GrubHub account.
HCI Concepts:

Affordances: Buttons like “More Transactions,” “Report Card Lost/Stolen,” and “Make a Deposit” clearly indicate their actions.

Example: The “Digital BuckID” button is large and centrally located, signaling that this is the main function for accessing this information.
Feedback: Successful scans at entrances trigger a sound, light or action.
Problem: Occasionally, users may need to use a different app to login (like the OSU Rec Sports app) to enter specific areas. That specific task requires logging in each time without it being saved.
Discoverability: Some features, like reporting a lost or stolen card, appear several times on different pages to make it very easy to access and find.
Error Prevention & Recovery: The app prevents invalid actions such as attempting a deposit without sufficient network connectivity and makes users login through a website to complete the action. This also provides an extra layer of security.
Consistency: Navigation is mostly consistent, with a bottom menu used for the main features (balances, transactions, QR code).
Issue: Some pages, like “Recent Transactions,” do not give enough information. Sometimes only one transaction may appear which can lead to confusion, given that there is no indicator of transactions disappearing after a certain amount of time, etc.
Learnability: Students quickly learn core functions like pulling up the QR code and checking balances, and advanced features like reporting a lost card or managing deposits don’t require repeated use to fully understand.
Constraints: The app restricts certain actions. For example, If a student wants to understand the difference between BuckID cash and Dining dollars, they are not able to access that immediately. Users would need to seek out that information on a different platform.
Cognitive Load: Multiple account balances, recent transactions, and menu options does not overwhelm new users. If people need to view more things and seek out help, they have the option to do that through a different platform. The help button is also indicated very clearly at the top of the interface when clicking through some channels.
The digital BuckID app is a convenient and essential tool for campus life, supporting multiple interactions, especially with account management. While it functions reliably, discoverability issues, and lack of additional integration reduces usability. A redesign could focus on more features, information, and connectivity with other physical channels on campus.
March 3, 2026
Coke Freestyle Machine (in dining halls)
Introduction / Context: The Coca-Cola Freestyle machine is a touchscreen soda fountain found in campus dining halls and cafés. It allows users to choose from dozens of base drinks and dozens of flavor combinations, dispense ice, and beverages for free. Interactions combine touchscreen input with physical placement of cups and sensors.

Concepts to explore: Affordances, feedback, discoverability, cognitive load, error prevention and recovery, mapping, constraints, and consistency.

The Coca-Cola Freestyle machine is an engaging and customizable beverage system that combines digital and physical interactions. While it provides a fun and modern experience, some things reduce its usability. A redesign could include clearer navigation, better grouped flavor categories, and faster feedback on selections. With these improvements, the system could be both fun and efficient, enhancing the overall user experience.
